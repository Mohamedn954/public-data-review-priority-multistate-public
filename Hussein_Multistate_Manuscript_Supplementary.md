## Appendix A: Oregon's Facility-Type Confounding Diagnosis

Section 3.4 reports that Oregon's shared-telephone clustering threshold required facility-type-aware recalibration. This appendix reports the full diagnostic evidence behind that conclusion: a categorization of all 49 raw shared-phone clusters identified in the Oregon analysis by whether the paired facilities shared a physical address and by facility type.

| Tier | Definition | Count | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| A: Campus Pairs | Same physical address, non-AFH facility types | 25 | Co-located license splits on a single campus (e.g., a licensed assisted-living wing paired with a separately licensed memory-care wing at the same street address). Not indicative of multi-site operator concentration; a structural artifact of how Oregon licenses distinct care levels within one physical development. |
| B: Brand Pairs/Clusters | Different addresses, non-AFH facility types, shared operating-brand naming | 13 | Likely reflects a multi-building senior-living campus or a single corporate brand operating adjoining or nearby developments. Ambiguous without ownership records. Includes the single five-license cluster responsible for every RF-08 flag. |
| C: AFH Operator Pairs | Different addresses, Adult Foster Home type, same individual operator of record on both licenses | 9 | The closest Oregon analogue to the construct the framework was built to detect: an individual licensed to operate more than one home under their own name. The fairest like-for-like comparison to the Washington pilot population. |
| D: Unexplained AFH Pairs | Different addresses, Adult Foster Home type, different named operators, sharing one telephone number | 2 | The most analytically interesting residual category: two separately named AFH operators, at two different addresses, sharing one administrative telephone number, with no address- or name-based explanation apparent from public data alone. |

Of the 49 raw clusters, 11 (Tiers C and D) resemble the intended multi-site-operator construct the framework targets. The remaining 38 (Tiers A and B), including the single five-license cluster that produces every RF-08 flag, appear more consistent with Oregon's facility-licensing and branding structure than with an undisclosed operator-concentration signal. This is the specific, falsifiable evidence behind the recalibration claim in Section 3.4: not an assertion that the threshold needed adjustment, but a categorized accounting of why.

The same recalibration question was also tested across a documented threshold range, rather than searched for a value that reproduced a targeted result:

| Threshold (shared licenses) | All facility types (n = 792) | AFH-only (n = 629) |
|---|---|---|
| 2 or more | 101 facilities (12.8%) | 22 facilities (3.5%), 11 distinct clusters |
| 3 or more (RF-08, as calibrated on Washington) | 5 facilities (0.6%) | 0 facilities |
| 4 or more | 5 facilities (0.6%) | 0 facilities |
| 5 or more | 5 facilities (0.6%) | 0 facilities |

From threshold three upward, the all-facility-type curve is carried entirely by the single five-license Tier B cluster. The AFH-only curve never reaches the three-license threshold in this two-county dataset. Restricting the comparison to the 629-facility Adult Foster Home subset, the facility type matching Washington's pilot population, identifies a pair-level sensitivity pattern at the two-license threshold, which Section 3.4 characterizes as a sensitivity result, not a validated threshold.

### Exploratory comparison: enforcement/investigation prevalence, clustered vs. nonclustered facilities

This is an exploratory, hypothesis-generating comparison, not a predictive-validity test — predictive validity remains untested for every indicator in this framework (Section 2.6). It asks a narrower question than the confounding diagnosis above: among facilities not restricted to the RF-08 threshold, does public enforcement or investigation prevalence differ between facilities in *any* shared-contact cluster and facilities in none, and does that comparison depend on the facility-type or county composition of who happens to be clustered? Computed via `scripts/build_cluster_enforcement_exploratory.py`: 2×2 tables (clustered vs. not) × (≥1 enforcement action / ≥1 investigation), crude and Mantel-Haenszel-adjusted (stratified by County for Washington, by facility type for Oregon), each run twice — on all facilities and again excluding facilities with no observable public record (`n_docs_total == 0`) — and reported with absolute prevalence, risk ratio, and odds ratio, each with a 95% CI, regardless of direction.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Washington | Enforcement | No | 164 / 6.7% | 3,293 / 4.7% | 1.43 (0.79–2.59) | 1.43 (0.79–2.60) |
| Washington | Enforcement | Yes | 118 / 9.3% | 2,052 / 7.5% | 1.24 (0.69–2.23) | 1.23 (0.68–2.20) |
| Washington | Investigation | No | 164 / 17.1% | 3,293 / 16.9% | 1.01 (0.71–1.42) | 1.01 (0.71–1.42) |
| Washington | Investigation | Yes | 118 / 23.7% | 2,052 / 27.2% | 0.87 (0.63–1.22) | 0.86 (0.62–1.20) |
| Oregon | Enforcement | No | 101 / 34.7% | 691 / 16.5% | **2.10 (1.53–2.88)** | **0.66 (0.48–0.92)** |
| Oregon | Enforcement | Yes | 99 / 35.4% | 575 / 19.8% | 1.78 (1.30–2.44) | 0.64 (0.46–0.89) |

| Oregon | Investigation | Yes | 99 / 55.6% | 575 / 28.9% | 1.92 (1.55–2.39) | 1.11 (0.87–1.42) |

Full stratum-level 2×2 tables, odds ratios, and both record-filter conditions are in `anonymized_data/cluster_enforcement_exploratory_detailed.csv` and `_summary.csv`.

Washington's comparison is essentially unchanged by county adjustment — crude and adjusted estimates are nearly identical for both outcomes, all confidence intervals crossing 1. Oregon's is not: the crude enforcement comparison shows clustered facilities more than twice as likely to carry an enforcement action (RR 2.10, 95% CI 1.53–2.88) — but stratified by facility type, this reverses to a statistically significant *lower* prevalence among clustered facilities (RR 0.66, 95% CI 0.48–0.92). The mechanism is directly visible in the stratum-level data: within every one of Oregon's four facility types individually, clustered facilities show lower or comparable enforcement prevalence than nonclustered facilities of the same type (0 of 22 vs. 66 of 607 for Adult Foster Homes; 13 of 33 vs. 17 of 27 for Assisted Living; 1 of 3 vs. 8 of 19 for Nursing Facilities; 21 of 43 vs. 23 of 38 for Residential Care Facilities) — but because clustered facilities are disproportionately concentrated in Oregon's higher-baseline-enforcement facility types (Assisted Living, Residential Care) rather than its lower-baseline-enforcement type (Adult Foster Home), the pooled crude comparison mechanically inherits that composition difference and points the opposite direction from every within-type comparison.

This is not evidence that shared-contact clustering identifies "safer" facilities, and this appendix does not make that claim — the direction, magnitude, and even statistical significance of this comparison changes depending on whether facility-type composition is accounted for, which is itself the finding: an unadjusted association between a structural indicator and an oversight outcome can be an artifact of population composition rather than a property of the indicator, precisely the population-validity distinction Section 2.6 defines and Section 3.4 documents for the clustering threshold itself. No claim in the main manuscript relies on this comparison; it is reported here as a further, independent illustration of the same lesson, not a new result requiring a change to any indicator's disposition.

## Appendix C: Oregon's System-Level Oversight Evidence, in Full

Section 1.1 and 3.2 report Oregon's SOQ evaluation headline figures (8% survey-timeliness compliance, 4,000+ case backlog). The full evaluation traces a four-stage chronology, reported here in full because the sequence itself, not only the headline numbers, is part of the evidentiary basis for classifying RF-16, RF-18, and RF-20 as System Supported.

**Identified problem** (February 2025). The first deliverable found the unit responsible for Assisted Living and Residential Care Facility licensing completed statutory two-year renewal surveys for only 8% of active facilities examined, and Nursing Facility survey compliance with its one-year window was 4.3%. It documented a complaint-investigation backlog exceeding 4,000 cases, with fewer than 200 closed in the month examined, and reliance on informal "Letters of Agreement" in place of formal licensing conditions Oregon statute requires, including for some findings of immediate jeopardy (Oregon Department of Human Services, 2025a).

**Formal response** (documented in the June 2025 follow-up, reviewing more than 220 legislative mandates, 30 Oregon Administrative Rules, and 115 staff surveys). Oregon's documented response included revision of Immediate Jeopardy decision protocols, a stated intent to clarify Letters of Agreement policy, statutory-requirement training for staff, workload and staffing realignment, a new performance-measurement structure, and active work items within the Licensing Complaint Unit covering complaint triage and a suspicious-death procedure (Oregon Department of Human Services, 2025b).

**Follow-up assessment milestone.** The June 2025 deliverable is itself the milestone: a second, independently commissioned, more granular assessment that documented planned remediation and several initiated work items rather than letting the process stall after the initial diagnosis, with an explicit remediation estimate of roughly one year to rebuild the unit's core structures (Oregon Department of Human Services, 2025b).

**Remaining outcome gap.** No third deliverable documenting the rebuild's outcome had been published as of this manuscript's snapshot date, and the June 2025 report itself still found the licensing unit lacking a shared understanding of organizational responsibilities and not routinely meeting survey and complaint-investigation timelines (Oregon Department of Human Services, 2025b). The corrective-action plan is reported here as documented and adopted in principle, not as completed in practice.

## Appendix D: Additional Washington System-Level Evidence

Beyond the HHS-OIG compliance audit reported in Section 3.3, Washington's own oversight infrastructure has been independently assessed multiple times, evidence this manuscript's main text does not have room to detail but which provides additional evidence of reactive or incomplete program-integrity controls from related perspectives.

The Washington State Auditor's Office found the same posture in two prior performance audits: its 2021 audit of the Health Care Authority's program-integrity function, and its 2023 audit of Medicaid managed-care program-integrity efforts, both describing oversight as reactive and complaint-driven rather than proactive and risk-based (Washington State Auditor's Office, 2021, 2023). A more recent state audit found more than $5.6 million in inappropriate Medicaid payments and documented internal-control deficiencies related to provider eligibility and managed-care oversight (State of Washington, Office of Financial Management, 2026). A joint review by the Washington State Auditor's Office and the HHS Office of Inspector General separately identified deficiencies in concurrent Medicaid enrollment eligibility integrity (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Office of Inspector General, 2025). And, as of the original Washington pilot's writing, the State Auditor's Office had an active performance audit underway examining more than 600 DSHS vendors whose spending showed patterns of exponential growth between 2021 and 2025 (Washington State Auditor's Office, 2026); this active audit is cited only as evidence of ongoing oversight attention to vendor spending growth, since no findings had been issued as of this manuscript's snapshot date.

Separately, a March 2026 federal advisory documented a 330% increase in Bank Secrecy Act filings related to healthcare fraud between 2020 and 2025, flagging the use of shell companies and straw owners to conceal beneficial ownership, a concealment pattern relevant to RF-07's ownership-transparency construct (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network, 2026).

## Appendix F: Additional Washington Methodological Detail

Section 3.3 reports Washington's headline structural counts. This appendix reports supporting methodological detail from the original Washington pilot that strengthens confidence in those counts but did not fit the main text.

**Data completeness.** Of the 3,457 pilot facilities, 1,287 (37.2%) had no public record of any kind, no inspection, relicensing action, complaint investigation, or enforcement action, within the study's available public-record window. This is a data-visibility limit, not evidence that those facilities had no oversight history; it is reported as a documented gap, consistent with how this manuscript treats Oregon's own missingness in Section 3.4.

**Independent corroboration of shared-contact clusters.** Of the 72 Washington shared-contact clusters, 70 (97.2%) had at least one additional administrative linkage beyond the shared telephone number: a shared DSHS administrative contact, a shared Secretary of State registered agent or principal-office address, a shared corporate governor, or a shared mailing address. Of those 70, only 26 clusters (14 via a shared Secretary of State registered agent, 12 via shared corporate governors), 36.1% of the 72 total clusters, were corroborated through a source independent of the licensing system itself; the remaining linkages drew on the same DSHS database the phone-based clustering came from, and so provide confirmatory but not independent support. This cross-check does not establish beneficial ownership; the shared-contact proxy was accompanied by at least one additional administrative linkage in most clusters, reducing but not eliminating the possibility of incidental number-sharing.

**Enforcement severity and county-level detail.** Of the 165 Washington facilities with at least one public enforcement action, 60 facilities—1.7% of the full 3,457-facility sample—had two or more enforcement actions, and 18 facilities (0.5% of the full sample) received a stop-placement order, the most severe action recorded. Within the three-county pilot, shared-contact cluster share ranged from 3.6% in Spokane County (22 of 610 facilities) to 5.3% in King County (95 of 1,795), with Pierce County at 4.5% (47 of 1,052). Pierce County separately showed the highest facility density per senior (approximately 7.2 per 1,000 residents 65 and older) alongside the highest enforcement (5.8%) and complaint (approximately 18%) rates of the three counties. This descriptive co-occurrence was not tested for statistical association and should not be interpreted as evidence that density caused greater enforcement activity. Combining cluster membership and enforcement-action flags, 318 facilities (9.2% of the pilot universe) were flagged by at least one of the two signals.

### Second-coder reliability check

Because this derivation audit was otherwise conducted by a single author, an independent second human coder (see Acknowledgments) was given only the three source documents and a blinded list of the 21 active constructs — no access to this Table, this manuscript, or the author's original classification — and separately classified all 21 rows. Raw agreement was **59.1% (13 of 22 items, including the retired RF-02)**, reported exactly as computed; no chance-corrected statistic is reported, since a 22-item comparison against a single rater does not support a stable kappa estimate. This figure is disclosed as the manuscript's actual inter-rater reliability, not a higher or more favorable one.

Three of the second coder's disagreements were not interpretive — they identified passages the original derivation audit's keyword search had missed entirely. ### False-positive safeguards

Every indicator with Limited convergence or better carries a documented benign explanation, an exclusion rule, a minimum-corroboration requirement, and a prohibited interpretation—the same discipline this manuscript already applies to RF-08's shared-contact clusters (Section 3.4; Appendix A). No public-data indicator in this framework licenses an adverse inference on its own.

| RF | Benign explanation(s) | Exclusion rule | Minimum corroboration required | Prohibited interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| RF-06 | Data-entry lag between a provider's exclusion in one state and another state's screening cycle; a common, non-fraudulent surname or entity-name collision | A single missed screening cycle, alone, does not establish evasion | An independent record showing continued billing *after* the exclusion date, not merely continued licensure | Must not be read as proof the provider knew of the exclusion or acted to evade it |
| RF-07 / RF-08 | Common management company, shared consultant, centralized answering service, franchise arrangement, or a data-entry artifact | A shared *commercial* registered agent alone is insufficient (a commercial agent may represent hundreds of unrelated entities) | A shared *individual* registered agent plus a common officer, address, or brand; or same-legal-entity confirmation | Must not be read as evidence of concealed ownership or misconduct absent independent corroboration of common control |
| RF-13 | A documented clerical or systems delay between a beneficiary's death/discharge and a state's eligibility-file update | A single isolated claim near a transition date, alone, does not establish intent | A pattern of repeated post-ineligibility billing, or an independent audit/case finding | Must not be read as proof of deliberate fraud absent a completed adjudicative finding |
| RF-14A | Genuine records-retention gaps unrelated to concealment; a documentation requirement that did not exist for a given service type at the time | Absence of a requirement is not equivalent to a violation of one | A documented finding that a requirement existed and was not met | Must not be read as evidence of fabrication (see RF-14B) |

### Portability status

This manuscript distinguishes six validity dimensions (Section 2.6): source, content, measurement, population, threshold, and predictive. RF-08 is the only indicator tested across four of the six, and it is where this framework's central population-validity finding comes from (Section 3.4): the pipeline transferred (technical reproduction), but the threshold required recalibration once facility type was held constant, because Oregon's mixed four-type population is not equivalent to Washington's single-type population — a population-validity finding, not merely a threshold-validity one. The new sensitivity analysis (`scripts/build_sensitivity_analysis.py`) extends this finding: Oregon's threshold-3 signal is carried almost entirely by its Residential Care Facility subtype (5 of 81 RCF facilities, 6.2%), not by Adult Family Homes (0 of 629 at threshold 3) — the same construction rule produces a different substantive result depending on which population it is applied to, independent of any threshold change. The new linkage-robustness analysis (`scripts/build_linkage_robustness.py`) adds a measurement-validity finding for the same indicator, with one correction the verification process itself caught before it could propagate: phone-key normalization is fully robust in Oregon (identical facility counts across four independent normalization variants) and, in Washington, robust once the pre-existing, deliberate exclusion of one non-facility-specific contact number is correctly carried forward from the primary data construction (50 facilities at threshold 3); a naive reconstruction that omits that one exclusion overcounts by exactly three facilities (53) and is reported only as a labeled sensitivity variant, not as evidence that phone-based measurement is unstable. Address-based linkage, by contrast, is genuinely sensitive to normalization choice (Oregon: 59 facilities under strict matching versus 71 under lenient matching at threshold 2, a 20% difference) — meaning RF-08's phone-based construction is the more measurement-stable of its two possible linkage bases, and the one apparent phone-based discrepancy traces to a single, fully diagnosed data-construction decision rather than to the normalization method itself.

No other indicator in this framework has been operationalized in a form that permits a technical/construct/threshold portability test; RF-13 and RF-06/RF-17B's Strong external convergence establishes that the underlying *vulnerability* recurs across jurisdictions, which is a different claim from establishing that a specific *measurement* of it would transfer without recalibration.

### Validation requirements

Grouped by what would change each remaining disposition, rather than repeated per row: RF-14B and RF-15 require the underlying court record for the one Washington case already identified. RF-07 and RF-09 require a Tier 1 source in the exact Medicaid long-term-care setting, since the sources found so far are either cross-program (Medicare) or below the evidentiary tier this framework treats as sufficient (an academic paper, for RF-07). RF-01, RF-03, RF-10, RF-11, and RF-19 require a qualifying source that has not yet been found despite a completed search and may not exist; their disposition would not change without one. No indicator's predictive validity—whether flagging it actually distinguishes confirmed problem facilities from benign ones—has been tested at any stage of this manuscript; that is Section 4.5's stated next step for the research program this manuscript begins, not a gap this appendix can close.

### Oregon's shared-contact corroboration (RF-08)

Unlike the sources in Tables H1–H2, RF-08 was tested directly rather than searched for in the secondary literature: all 49 of Oregon's shared-telephone-number facility clusters were individually checked against Oregon's public business registry. Thirty-six clusters (73%) reflected an affiliation already visible in the facility names themselves (e.g., a shared assisted-living brand operating adjoining assisted-living and memory-care licenses); eleven additional clusters (22%)—not visible from the facility names at all—were corroborated through registry evidence: eight through a common officer, owner, or manager, two through a common residential or business address (kept analytically distinct, since a shared address alone does not establish common control), and one through outright common legal-entity identity. One cluster (2%) yielded no corroborating public connection, and one (2%) remained indeterminate because the operator's surname was too common to confidently resolve identity in the registry. The eleven non-obvious corroborations are the result that matters for RF-08's standing as an entity-resolution proxy: they demonstrate the shared-contact signal surfacing real relationships the public-facing facility data does not itself disclose, while the one non-corroborated and one indeterminate cluster demonstrate why the proxy requires case-level corroboration rather than standing on its own as a review-priority finding. Full cluster-level detail, evidence-class coding, and the registry citations underlying each corroborated relationship are in the released anonymized data (`anonymized_data/oregon/A5_OR_Cluster_Corroboration_ANON.csv`); the raw facility names and registry records reviewed to produce that classification are not part of the public replication package, consistent with this manuscript's general treatment of privacy-sensitive entity-resolution steps (Section 2.6).
